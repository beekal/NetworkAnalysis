      Analysis: data mining doesn’t work for spotting terrorists

      A comprehensive report from the National Research Council makes an iron-clad …

      by Jon Stokes - Oct 9 2008, 8:02pm NST

      • Share
      • Tweet
      • Google
      • Reddit

      "Automated identification of terrorists through data mining (or any other known methodology) is neither feasible as an objective nor desirable as a goal of technology development efforts."
      —Protecting Individual Privacy in the Struggle Against Terrorists: A Framework for Program Assessment, pp. 27-28

      Earlier this week, the National Research Council produced a remarkably thorough 376-page report on data mining, counter-terrorism, and American democracy. I've plucked the quote above from the report's executive summary not only because it aptly summarizes both the report's implications and my own near-decade of thinking about and reporting on these efforts. More significantly, it runs directly counter to the prevailing wisdom in Washington on how best to use technology to defend the US homeland against a catastrophic terrorist attack.

      The report makes an iron-clad case that data mining might be an effective counter-terrorism tool if used judiciously, but it is not the silver bullet that the architects of Total Information Awareness and its relatives believe it to be. In short, betting the safety of the country on data mining is effectively a bet on the operational incompetence of terrorists. And, given what we know of our enemies' increasing sophistication, that's not a bet that we should make.

      I won't attempt to summarize the report, or even list the highlights from it, in this short article. Instead, I'll remix some of the themes and examples in the report to paint my own picture of what's wrong with the "let's vacuum up all the world's information into giant computers and mine it for terrorist clues" mentality that has taken over our national defense establishment in the wake of 9/11. (In fairness to Bush, Clinton got the ball rolling here. Bush just loaded the ball into a cannon and lit the fuse.)

      The problem: preventing a crime by someone with no criminal record

      In the counter-terrorism panel at the University of Chicago that I covered in 2006, former federal anti-terrorism prosecutor Andrew McCarthy raised a point that I've been grappling with ever since. In his talk, McCarthy made a very good case against framing counter-terror efforts as a law enforcement problem. The distinction that McCarthy drew between traditional law enforcement efforts and counter-terror efforts is fundamental and undeniable: criminal prosecutors and judges are concerned with determining the guilt or innocence of a suspect in the wake of an already-committed crime; counter-terror officials are concerned with preventing crimes from occurring by identifying suspects before they've done anything wrong.

      Think about it. Unlike a mobster in a conspiracy-to-commit trial (another instance in which planning to commit a crime is itself a crime), a suicide bomber has no record of repeated suicide bombings. In fact, most terrorists have no criminal record of any kind that could bring them to the attention of authorities or work against them in court.

      Given that most terrorists are unremarkable and law-abiding up until the very moment that they detonate themselves in an act of mass carnage, it's impossible to predict an individual terrorist's next move based on previous bad acts. Counter-terror experts are keenly aware of this dilemma, which is why they've pinned their hopes on using data mining tools to identify terrorist behaviors—individual and collective behavior patterns that could tip an agent off to a possible terror plot.

      Data mining has had remarkable success in predicting consumer behavior for advertising and credit card reporting agencies, and many of the folks tasked with keeping us safe believe that they can replicate that success with terrorists. Unfortunately, they can't.

      The typical terrorist?

      In various places, the NRC report uses as a contrastive example one of the most successful implementations of data mining in the modern marketplace: credit reporting. Agencies like Equifax can predict an individual's likelihood of delinquency with a very high degree of confidence by comparing her credit history with the credit histories of millions of other consumers.

      But, just like terrorists aren't the same as mobsters with criminal records, they also aren't the same as consumers with credit histories. There's no large dataset of terrorist behavior out there that can be used to train a data mining application to predict an individual's intention to commit an act of terror with any degree of confidence.

      In credit scoring and in advertising, the predictive capability of data mining stems from its ability to construct generalities about what's "normal" or "typical" for a given training dataset, so this lack of a body of terrorist behavior data is really the biggest barrier to using data mining to identify future terror perpetrators by their present actions.

      But there's another barrier to the effective use of data mining in counter-terrorism, and it's almost as big.

      Garbage in, garbage out

      As the NRC report points out, not only is the training data lacking, but the input data that you'd actually be mining has been purposely corrupted by the terrorists themselves. Terrorist plotters actively disguise their activities using operational security measures (opsec) like code words, encryption, and other forms of covert communication. So, even if we had access to a copious and pristine body of training data that we could use to generalize about the "typical terrorist," the new data that's coming into the data mining system is suspect.

      To return to the credit reporting analogy, credit scores would be worthless to lenders if everyone could manipulate their credit history (e.g., hide past delinquencies) the way that terrorists can manipulate the data trails that they leave as they buy gas, enter buildings, make phone calls, surf the Internet, etc.

      So this application of data mining bumps up against the classic GIGO (garbage in, garbage out) problem in computing, with the terrorists deliberately feeding the system garbage. What this means in real-world terms is that the success of our counter-terrorism data mining efforts is completely dependent on the failure of terrorist cells to maintain operational security.

      The combination of the GIGO problem and the lack of suitable training data combine to make big investments in automated terrorist identification a futile and wasteful effort. Furthermore, these two problems are structural, so they're not going away. All legitimate concerns about false positives and corrosive effects on civil liberties aside, data mining will never give authorities the ability to identify terrorists or terrorist networks with any degree of confidence.

      But, until our lawmakers really figure this out, they'll continue throwing money and computers at the problem, and we won't be any safer for it.
